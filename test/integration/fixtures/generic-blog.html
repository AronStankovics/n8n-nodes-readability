How I organise my notes

Posted by Generic Blogger on a sunny Tuesday

People ask me often how I keep track of the hundreds of little notes I jot down every week — from meeting minutes and research threads to tiny sparks of ideas that might one day become bigger projects. The honest answer is that I have tried almost every system popular over the past decade, and what has survived is not any of the systems on their own but the habits I've built around switching between them deliberately.

First, everything goes into a daily file. The daily file is just a plain-text document named by date, and it lives in a flat folder that holds one file per day. Nothing about that is novel; what has made it stick for me is an ironclad rule against ever going back and reformatting old daily files. They are a scratchpad; they are not a knowledge base. If I find I want to keep something, I lift it out into a dedicated file.

Dedicated files live in a separate, topic-oriented folder. Each file has a short descriptive name and a single responsibility: a person, a project, a recurring question. When two topics start overlapping, I either merge the files or, more often, split them more finely. This matters surprisingly a lot. My hypothesis is that the right granularity is whatever granularity matches the questions you actually ask later. Related reading is helpful here — several writers have made this point far better than I can.

The final layer is what I call the "garden" — a small number of long-form notes that I actively rewrite and link together. These are the ones I would happily share publicly, and in some cases do. I've found that the act of polishing a note for public consumption forces me to understand the topic in a way that merely collecting quotes never does. If you take one thing from this post, make it this: publish, even quietly, because publishing is how you know you have thought something through.

There's more I could say — tooling, weekly reviews, search — but that's enough for today. Reach out if you'd like to compare notes. I'm always curious how other people do this.
