Weekly Newsletter - Episode 42

By A. Writer

Welcome back to the weekly newsletter. This week we cover a handful of themes that have been circulating among readers: long-form journalism renaissance, the quiet revival of the plain RSS feed, and the ever-present tension between web pages designed for attention and web pages designed for comprehension. Settle in with your favourite beverage — there is a lot to unpack.

First, a note on long-form. Several major outlets have quietly expanded their long-form teams this quarter, in apparent response to reader demand for pieces that go beyond the five-paragraph brief. Editors whom I spoke with cited a measurable increase in time-on-page for investigations compared with daily briefings, and pointed to a surprising bump in paid subscriptions following long-form releases. If you had assumed the long-form essay was on the way out, the numbers tell a different story.

The RSS revival, meanwhile, is harder to measure, but every month brings new tools, new readers, and small improvements to the plumbing of the open web. It is nothing like the old days, but it is very much alive. For a taste, see the embedded demonstration below, which shows how a modern reader resolves and renders a feed entry.

Finally, the attention-versus-comprehension tension is, I think, the most interesting story of the three. As the attention economy moves toward ever shorter and more spectacular formats, comprehension-oriented products have carved out a small but real niche. Browsers now ship with reader modes. E-readers have never been more popular with bibliophiles. Even newsletters — the format you are reading now — have become a kind of protest against the infinite feed.

Thanks, as always, for reading. Forward this to a friend, reply with comments, and I will see you next week.
